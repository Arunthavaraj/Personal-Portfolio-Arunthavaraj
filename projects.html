Unsupervised Machine Learning of Rainfall (dissertation)

This dissertation employs unsupervised machine learning techniques to analyse the high resolution spatiotemporal rainfall data in the United Kingdom region, briefly covering the period from 1980 to 2080. The modelling was implemented to classify the traditional classification of rainfall as convective or frontal whenever new rainfall data was applied. Over this, the transition of rainfall from one type to another and new patterns were discussed. DBSCAN was employed in the dataset incorporating Grid Search algorithm and secondary characteristics were derived. To increase the accuracy, random sampling of training data with 10-day intervals was selected and a model was built using DBSCAN and Hierarchical clustering. Further, to predict the new data point, a KNN classifier was employed. Also, sub-dividing the hierarchical clusters yielded even more segregation of traditional types of rainfall. The results were satisfactory as new data points were predicted with greater accuracy and new insights were derived. This approach demonstrated the potential of machine learning algorithms in increasing accuracy, deducing newer patterns, and understanding cloud precipitation.

Customer Churn Prediction

This machine learning-based classification model aims to help banks predict which customers are likely to cancel their accounts. By identifying the factors that lead to churn, banks can take proactive steps to retain valuable clients and improve profitability. Customer churn is a significant concern in the banking industry, as retaining existing customers is far more cost-effective than acquiring new ones. This project uses a simulated Kaggle dataset, representing real-world banking scenarios, to build a predictive model that identifies key factors driving customer churn.
